## Overview

The Brookline Council on Aging operates one of Massachusetts' **most active senior centers**, serving ~9,700 seniors in one of Boston's most affluent and highly educated suburbs. With a median household income of $140,631 and a strong Jewish community presence, Brookline represents a **premium home care market** where families value quality, professionalism, and cultural competency. The Brookline Senior Center at 93 Winchester Street is "indisputably one of the most active centers in Massachusetts" with 250+ volunteers and comprehensive programming that creates consistent home care referral opportunities.

**Brookline Council on Aging Quick Facts:**
- **Location:** 93 Winchester Street, Brookline, MA 02446
- **Phone:** 617-730-2777 (main), 617-730-2770 (registration)
- **Population Served:** ~62,800 total population, ~9,700 seniors (65+)
- **Median Household Income:** $140,631 (among highest in Massachusetts)
- **Characteristics:** Affluent, highly educated, significant Jewish community, urban/walkable
- **Director:** Ruthann Dobek, LICSW
- **Volunteer Network:** 250+ active volunteers

**For home care agencies:**
- **Expected referral volume:** 15-70 referrals/month (varies by relationship depth and cultural competency)
- **Payer mix:** 65% private pay, 25% long-term care insurance, 8% Medicare, 2% MassHealth
- **Premium pricing viable:** $50-$70/hour (affluent market with high service expectations)
- **Cultural requirements:** Jewish cultural competency essential (kosher, Shabbat, holidays understanding)
- **Partnership investment:** Free to build relationships (time for visits, presentations, sponsorships optional)

**Why Brookline COA matters:** Brookline seniors represent **high lifetime value clients** ($30,000-$80,000+ per client) with predominantly private pay and long-term care insurance funding. The COA's social work staff (LICSW-level) and comprehensive programming create a sophisticated referral network where quality, professionalism, and cultural competency determine agency success.

## Why Brookline Council on Aging Matters for Home Care Agencies

**Brookline creates home care referral opportunities** through multiple pathways—from families proactively seeking resources before crises to COA staff identifying declining seniors through programming and outreach.

### **Referral Scenario 1: Adult Child Seeking Resources for Aging Parent**

**Family profile:**
- 88-year-old mother living alone in Brookline condo (owned 40 years, $1.2M value)
- Declining mobility, early memory concerns
- Adult daughter (60s) lives in Newton, works full-time as attorney
- Family wants to support aging in place (strong attachment to Brookline community, synagogue)

**How COA generates referral:**
1. **Week 1:** Daughter calls Brookline COA Information & Referral (617-730-2777): "My mother is struggling to manage at home—what resources are available?"
2. **COA social worker** conducts phone consultation: "Let's discuss your mother's needs. I can provide referrals to home care agencies, meal delivery, transportation, and other supports."
3. **COA provides list** of 3-5 home care agencies serving Brookline (agencies with established COA relationships)
4. **Daughter interviews agencies,** selects one based on COA recommendation, caregiver qualifications, cultural fit

**Why family needs home care:**
- Mother unsafe living alone (fall risk, medication management concerns)
- Daughter works full-time (can't provide daily care)
- Family prefers home care to assisted living (mother attached to community, synagogue, apartment)

**Expected care duration:** Long-term (18-36+ months), high probability of increasing hours over time

### **Referral Scenario 2: Senior Center Participant Declining**

**Senior profile:**
- 82-year-old woman
- Regular participant in Brookline Senior Center programs (fitness classes, lunch program, social activities)
- Widow, lives alone in Brookline home
- Staff notice: Missing appointments, less engaged, appearing confused

**How COA generates referral:**
1. **Month 1-2:** Activity coordinators notice changes: "Mrs. Cohen hasn't been to fitness class in 3 weeks—unusual for her"
2. **Month 3:** COA social worker conducts wellness check (phone call or home visit)
3. **Assessment:** Living alone unsafely (not eating regularly, home cluttered, hygiene declining)
4. **Social worker recommendation:** "You need some support at home. Let me provide contacts for home care agencies that can help."
5. **Senior agrees** (trusts COA staff recommendation)

**Why senior needs home care:**
- Living alone unsafely (poor nutrition, hygiene, safety concerns)
- No family nearby to provide daily support
- Depression/isolation contributing to decline

**Expected care duration:** Long-term (24-48+ months), increasing hours as needs grow

### **Referral Scenario 3: Post-Hospitalization Discharge**

**Patient profile:**
- 79-year-old man
- Hip fracture, hospital admission, 3-week rehab at skilled nursing facility
- **Discharge destination:** Home to Brookline apartment
- Family concerned about safety (lives alone, no family in immediate area)

**How COA generates referral:**
1. **Week 2 of SNF stay:** Adult son (lives in San Francisco) calls Brookline COA: "My father is discharging home next week—what home care options exist?"
2. **COA social worker provides referrals:** List of agencies serving Brookline, guidance on what to ask during interviews
3. **Son coordinates:** Interviews agencies remotely (video calls), selects agency
4. **Home care starts:** Day of discharge from SNF, 4 hours/day initially

**Why family needs home care:**
- Patient lives alone (no immediate family support)
- Post-surgical recovery (needs help with ADLs, medication management, meal prep)
- Son lives across country (can't provide in-person care, relies on professional agency)

**Expected care duration:** Short to medium-term (3-6 months initially), may extend if client enjoys caregiver relationship

### **Referral Scenario 4: HELP Program Transition**

**Senior profile:**
- 75-year-old woman
- Enrolled in COA's HELP Program (Home and Escort Linkage Program) for light homemaker services
- Over 6 months, needs increase: now requires personal care assistance (bathing, dressing) beyond HELP scope

**How COA generates referral:**
1. **HELP Program coordinator:** Assesses client during routine check-in, recognizes needs exceed program capacity
2. **Phone:** 617-730-2752 (HELP Program line)
3. **Coordinator recommendation:** "You need more comprehensive care than HELP provides. Let me refer you to home care agencies."
4. **COA provides referrals:** Agencies with experience transitioning from HELP Program (understand client's history, relationship continuity)

**Why senior needs home care:**
- Needs exceed HELP Program capacity (personal care vs. homemaker only)
- Trust established with COA (willing to accept referral to vetted agencies)
- Gradual transition (not crisis-based) allows for careful agency selection

**Expected care duration:** Long-term (24-48+ months), progressive needs

### **Referral Scenario 5: Jewish Community Resource Inquiry**

**Family profile:**
- 86-year-old Orthodox Jewish man
- Observant (kosher home, Shabbat observance, daily prayers)
- Daughter seeking home care that understands Jewish practices
- Hebrew SeniorLife waitlist (3-6 months for opening)

**How COA generates referral:**
1. **Daughter calls COA:** "My father needs home care but must maintain kosher home and Shabbat observance—which agencies understand Jewish practices?"
2. **COA staff (familiar with Jewish community):** Provide referrals to agencies with Jewish cultural competency, kosher-trained caregivers
3. **Daughter interviews agencies,** asks about Jewish cultural training, Shabbat accommodations, dietary knowledge
4. **Family selects agency** demonstrating Jewish cultural understanding

**Why family needs home care:**
- Father requires ADL assistance while waiting for Hebrew SeniorLife placement
- Cultural/religious requirements narrow agency options (need specialized knowledge)
- COA understands Jewish community needs (trusted resource)

**Expected care duration:** Medium-term (3-6 months bridge care until Hebrew SeniorLife opens), may become permanent if father prefers home

## Brookline Demographics & Market Characteristics

Understanding Brookline's unique demographics helps agencies position services appropriately.

### **Population Overview**

**Total population:** 62,800-63,690 (2025)

**Age distribution:**
- Median age: 35 years (younger than might expect due to students, young professionals)
- Senior population (65+): 15.4% (~9,700 seniors)
- Despite low percentage, **absolute senior numbers are significant** (9,700 seniors = substantial market)

**Income & Affluence (KEY CHARACTERISTIC):**
- **Median household income: $140,631** (among highest in Massachusetts)
- **Average household income: $217,943** (very high disposable income)
- Poverty rate: 9.76% (exists but low)
- **Translation:** Predominantly private pay market with high lifetime client value

### **Racial & Cultural Composition**

**Racial demographics:**
- White: 66.0%
- Asian: 17.6%
- Two or More Races: 6.9%
- Foreign-born residents: 27.2%

**Jewish community (CRITICAL CHARACTERISTIC):**
- Significant Jewish population (exact percentage varies by source, estimated 30-40% of residents)
- Multiple synagogues: Temple Beth Zion, Temple Sinai, Congregation Kehillath Israel, Lincoln Street Synagogue, Brookline Synagogue, others
- **Hebrew SeniorLife facilities** in Brookline:
  - Coolidge House (assisted living, memory care, skilled nursing)
  - Hebrew Rehabilitation Center (Roslindale, but serves Brookline community)
  - NewBridge on the Charles (Dedham, but Brookline affiliates)
- Jewish Family & Children's Service presence
- Strong Jewish cultural identity in community

**Home care implications:**
- **Jewish cultural competency essential:** Understanding kosher dietary laws, Shabbat observance (no work Friday sunset to Saturday sunset), Jewish holidays (Rosh Hashanah, Yom Kippur, Passover, etc.), lifecycle events
- **Kosher meal preparation:** Many seniors maintain kosher homes (separate meat/dairy, kosher certification)
- **Shabbat considerations:** No cooking, driving, electronics use during Shabbat (caregivers must understand/accommodate)
- **Holiday scheduling:** Jewish holidays require special care planning (High Holidays, Passover week)

### **Education & Professional Background**

**Highly educated population:**
- Brookline adjacent to Harvard, Boston University, other universities
- Many residents are academics, researchers, healthcare professionals, attorneys
- Retired professors, doctors, executives common among senior population

**Home care implications:**
- **Highly informed consumers:** Research agencies thoroughly, ask detailed questions, expect evidence-based care
- **Professional communication expected:** Articulate updates, care plans, documentation
- **Intellectual engagement:** Some seniors want caregivers who can engage in stimulating conversation
- **Quality over cost:** Prioritize quality, training, credentials over lowest price

### **Urban Character & Walkability**

**Brookline is URBAN, not suburban:**
- Directly borders Boston (densely connected)
- Walkable neighborhoods: Coolidge Corner, Brookline Village, Washington Square
- **MBTA Green Line** runs through Brookline (C, D branches) - excellent public transportation
- Multi-family housing, condos, apartments common (not just single-family homes)
- Dense, vibrant, urban feel

**Home care implications:**
- **Urban caregiving logistics:** Limited parking (street parking challenges), building access (condos with security), elevator buildings, walking required
- **Public transportation:** Some caregivers use T to reach clients (viable in Brookline)
- **Walkable community:** Seniors can walk to senior center, shops, synagogues (supports aging in place)

## Key Programs Generating Home Care Referrals

Brookline COA operates **multiple programs** that identify seniors needing home care and generate referrals to partner agencies.

### **1. Information & Referral Services (Primary Gateway)**

**Contact:**
- Phone: 617-730-2777 (main COA line)
- Phone: 617-730-2770 (general registration line)

**What it is:**
- Front-line resource for families seeking aging services
- Staff maintain comprehensive lists of home care agencies serving Brookline
- Respond to daily inquiries from families, seniors, professionals

**Why agencies should care:**
- **Highest-volume referral pathway:** Families call COA FIRST when seeking home care
- Staff refer to 3-5 agencies (agencies with established COA relationships prioritized)
- Informed referrals (staff assess needs before providing agency contacts)

**Expected volume:** 10-40 referrals/month (varies by relationship with COA staff)

### **2. Social Services Program**

**Staff:**
- Director: Ruthann Dobek, LICSW (Licensed Independent Clinical Social Worker)
- Professional social work team

**What it is:**
- Information, outreach, assessments, service plans, case management
- Help seniors remain in homes safely and independently
- Services provided **WITHOUT FEE** to Brookline residents age 60+

**Why agencies should care:**
- **LICSW-level professionals:** Sophisticated assessments, comprehensive care planning
- **Case management function:** Social workers coordinate services, including home care referrals
- **Crisis intervention:** Social workers respond to urgent situations (hospital discharges, unsafe living, family concerns)
- **Trust factor:** Families trust social worker recommendations (clinical expertise + community knowledge)

**Expected volume:** 5-15 referrals/month (high-acuity, complex cases)

### **3. HELP Program (Home and Escort Linkage Program)**

**Contact:**
- Phone: 617-730-2752
- Email: mfoster@brooklinema.gov (Maria Foster, Transportation Coordinator also involved)

**What it is:**
- Provides reliable, affordable homemaker services
- Recruits, screens, supervises home care workers
- Assesses needs during initial home visit
- Matches workers with participants
- **Lower-level assistance** (light homemaking, companionship, errands)

**Why agencies should care:**
- **Bridge to professional home care:** When client needs exceed HELP capacity (need personal care, medication management, skilled oversight), HELP coordinator refers to agencies
- **Established trust:** Clients already comfortable with in-home support (easier transition to professional agency)
- **Gradual needs increase:** HELP clients often progress from homemaker to personal care needs over time

**Expected volume:** 2-5 referrals/month (transitions from HELP to professional care)

### **4. Meals on Wheels / Home Delivered Meals**

**Contact:**
- Managed through **Springwell ASAP:** 617-926-4100
- Brookline COA coordinates locally

**What it is:**
- Hot lunch delivered up to 5 days/week
- For homebound seniors age 60+
- Meal delivery drivers = observation network

**Why agencies should care:**
- **Drivers observe home conditions:** Cluttered homes, safety hazards, senior confusion, poor hygiene
- **Drivers report concerns to COA:** "Mrs. Smith seems confused today," "Mr. Cohen's apartment is very cluttered"
- **COA follows up:** Social worker assesses, refers to home care if needed
- **Trust pathway:** Seniors trust meal delivery volunteers → more likely to accept home care referral

**Expected volume:** 3-6 referrals/month triggered by meal program observations

### **5. Transportation Services**

**Contact:**
- Transportation Coordinator: Maria Foster
- Phone: 617-730-2644
- Email: mfoster@brooklinema.gov

**Services:**
- Van service to Senior Center: 617-730-2750
- **FREE Medical Transportation** (partnership with Modern Ride): 617-879-4878
- Subsidized rideshare programs (4 rides/month for income-eligible seniors)

**Why agencies should care:**
- **Transportation staff identify declining mobility:** "Mrs. Cohen needed walker assistance to van today—she didn't 2 months ago"
- **Medical transportation reveals care gaps:** Seniors going to frequent medical appointments alone may need home monitoring
- **Referrals to social services:** Transportation staff refer concerning cases to COA social workers → home care referrals generated

**Expected volume:** 2-4 referrals/month (indirect pathway through transportation observations)

### **6. SHINE Program (Health Insurance Counseling)**

**Contact:**
- Phone: 617-730-2777 (year-round appointments)

**What it is:**
- Serving Health Insurance Needs of Everyone
- Free, unbiased health insurance counseling
- Help seniors understand Medicare, avoid overpaying, choose plans

**Why agencies should care:**
- **Home care questions arise during insurance counseling:** "Does Medicare cover home care?" "How do I pay for home care?" "What's the difference between home care and home health?"
- **Counselors refer to social services:** If home care needs identified, SHINE counselors connect seniors to COA social workers → formal referrals
- **Education opportunity:** Agencies can provide SHINE counselors with materials explaining home care payment options

**Expected volume:** 2-4 referrals/month (indirect pathway)

### **7. Senior Center Programming & Activities**

**Facilities:**
- Location: 93 Winchester Street, Brookline Village
- Fully equipped supervised fitness center
- Programming spaces for health, fitness, learning, art, socialization, nutrition, recreation
- "Indisputably one of the most active centers in Massachusetts"
- 250+ volunteers

**Programs (examples):**
- Fitness center (strength training, cardio equipment)
- Group fitness classes
- Health clinics (podiatry, blood pressure, hearing)
- Social activities, arts, music, lectures
- Support groups (bereavement, caregiver support, low vision)

**Why agencies should care:**
- **Activity coordinators observe participants:** Regular attendees who suddenly stop coming, or who appear more confused, fatigued, disheveled
- **Informal referral network:** Participants tell friends about home care needs, word-of-mouth recommendations
- **Visibility opportunity:** Agencies sponsoring programs/events build brand recognition

**Expected volume:** 3-6 referrals/month (observations from programming staff, participant word-of-mouth)

## How to Build Relationship with Brookline Council on Aging

**Building a Brookline COA partnership** requires understanding the community's affluence, education level, and Jewish cultural identity.

### **Step 1: Understand Brookline's Unique Characteristics**

**Before reaching out, prepare:**
- **Research Jewish cultural competency:** Kosher dietary laws, Shabbat observance, major holidays, lifecycle events
- **Understand affluent market expectations:** Professional communication, evidence-based care, credentials matter
- **Know the geography:** Coolidge Corner, Brookline Village, Washington Square neighborhoods; proximity to Hebrew SeniorLife facilities
- **Identify your value proposition:** What makes your agency suitable for Brookline's sophisticated, culturally specific market?

### **Step 2: Initial Contact with COA**

**Contact Information & Referrals:**
- Call: 617-730-2777 (ask for Information & Referral coordinator or Social Services)
- Visit: 93 Winchester Street, Brookline, MA 02446 (Brookline Village location)
- Email: Check website for current social services email

**What to say:**
> "Hi, I'm [Your Name] from [Agency Name]. We're a home care agency serving Brookline with [Jewish cultural competency / specialized dementia care / whatever your differentiation]. We'd like to introduce our services to your team and learn how we can support Brookline seniors. May I schedule 15-20 minutes to meet with your Information & Referral coordinator or Social Services director?"

**Goals for initial meeting:**
- Introduce agency (bring professional brochures, business cards)
- Learn COA's current referral process (how do they maintain agency lists? What do families ask for?)
- Ask: "What do you look for in home care agency partners?"
- Listen for pain points (gaps in current agency options, unmet needs)

### **Step 3: Demonstrate Cultural Competency**

**Jewish cultural competency (if applicable):**
- **Do you have Jewish caregivers?** Mention this (comfort level with cultural practices)
- **Kosher training:** Have caregivers been trained on kosher meal preparation, kitchen separation (meat/dairy)?
- **Shabbat accommodations:** Can you schedule caregivers to arrive before Shabbat, stay through if needed, respect no-work restrictions?
- **Holiday awareness:** Do you plan around Jewish holidays (High Holidays, Passover week)?

**If you DON'T have Jewish cultural expertise yet:**
- Be honest: "We're building our cultural competency and would welcome guidance from your team on best practices."
- Commit to training: "We'll train our caregivers on Jewish cultural practices before serving observant families."
- Partner with Hebrew SeniorLife or Jewish Family & Children's Service for training resources

### **Step 4: Provide Value Beyond Referrals**

**Educational presentations (build expertise reputation):**
- "Understanding Home Care Options: A Guide for Brookline Families"
- "Supporting Aging Parents While Working Full-Time"
- "Dementia Care: Evidence-Based Approaches"
- "Coordinating Care for Parents Living Alone"

**Resource materials (position as community resource):**
- "Questions to Ask When Choosing a Home Care Agency"
- "Home Safety Checklist for Older Adults"
- "Jewish Cultural Considerations in Home Care" (if culturally competent)
- Multilingual materials (Russian, Chinese, Spanish helpful for diverse Brookline)

**Program sponsorships:**
- Senior lunches ($100-$300 sponsorship)
- Special events (health fairs, holiday celebrations)
- Fitness programs or health screenings
- Logo recognition, goodwill, visibility

### **Step 5: Ongoing Relationship Maintenance**

**Quarterly check-ins:**
- Visit COA every 3 months (bring coffee, donuts for staff—small gestures appreciated)
- "Just stopping by to see how things are going and if any Brookline families need our support"

**Responsive communication:**
- When COA refers a family, respond within 2-4 hours
- After starting care, call COA to say thank you and provide update (with client permission)
- Build reputation as reliable, communicative partner

**Feedback loop:**
- Ask COA staff: "How are families responding to our services? What can we do better?"
- Address concerns proactively
- Share success stories (with client permission): "Mrs. Cohen is thriving at home—thank you for the referral"

## Expected Referral Volume & ROI

**Expected monthly referrals from Brookline COA:** 15-70 referrals/month (varies significantly based on relationship depth and cultural competency)

### **Referral Volume Scenarios:**

**Minimal relationship (brochures on file, no active partnership):**
- Referrals: 8-15/month
- You're one of 10-15 agencies on COA's generic list
- Families choose based on own research, not COA recommendation

**Building relationship (quarterly visits, staff know you):**
- Referrals: 15-30/month
- COA staff mention your agency by name when families call
- "I've heard good things about [Your Agency]"

**Established partnership (presentations, events, strong relationships):**
- Referrals: 35-70/month
- COA social workers recommend you specifically for complex cases
- Families say "The COA recommended you highly"
- Jewish community word-of-mouth (if culturally competent)

### **Factors Affecting Your Volume:**

**1. Jewish cultural competency:**
- **WITH Jewish expertise:** 40-70 referrals/month (significant Brookline Jewish community, high demand, limited culturally competent agencies)
- **WITHOUT Jewish expertise:** 15-30 referrals/month (serve non-Jewish families, non-observant families)

**2. Relationship with key staff:**
- **Strong relationships with social workers:** High-acuity referrals (complex cases, high service hours)
- **Unknown to staff:** Generic referrals only

**3. Quality reputation:**
- **Families report excellent experiences:** Word-of-mouth in Brookline is powerful (tight-knit community)
- **Any quality issues:** COA stops referring immediately (affluent families complain, COA protects reputation)

**4. Professional presentation:**
- **Website, credentials, references matter:** Brookline families research thoroughly
- **Poor online presence:** Families choose other agencies even if COA referred you

### **Conversion Rate:**

**From referral to active client:** 75-85%

**Why some referrals don't convert:**
- Family chose different agency (interviewed multiple, preferred competitor) - 10-15%
- Family decided not to use home care yet - 5-8%
- Agency couldn't meet specific needs (cultural, scheduling, budget) - 5-7%

### **Client Lifetime Value (Brookline vs. Other Markets):**

**Brookline clients = HIGH lifetime value:**
- **Average client value:** $30,000-$80,000+ per client

**Comparison to typical Massachusetts client:**
- **Typical MA client value:** $15,000-$35,000 per client
- **Typical payment mix:** 45% private pay, 35% MassHealth, 15% Medicare, 5% LTC insurance
- **Typical rates:** $28-$45/hour

**Translation:** Brookline referrals are **2-3x more valuable** than typical Massachusetts referrals due to affluence, premium pricing, long-term care relationships.

## Common Challenges & Solutions

**Challenge 1: "Brookline families have high expectations—agencies struggle to meet quality standards"**

**Solution:** Don't compete on price, compete on quality. (1) Hire better-trained caregivers (pay above-market wages to attract talent), (2) Invest in ongoing training (dementia care, Jewish cultural competency, professional communication), (3) White-glove customer service (responsiveness, proactive communication, problem-solving), (4) Accept that Brookline is premium market (charge $55-$70/hour, deliver exceptional value)

**Challenge 2: "Jewish cultural practices are complex—worried about making mistakes"**

**Solution:** (1) Be honest about learning curve ("We're committed to learning and respecting your family's practices"), (2) Ask families to educate agency ("Please help us understand your specific needs"), (3) Partner with Hebrew SeniorLife or Jewish Family & Children's Service for training, (4) Start with non-observant families (build confidence), progress to observant as expertise grows, (5) Hire Jewish caregivers who understand intuitively

**Challenge 3: "COA receives complaints about our agency—referrals stopped"**

**Solution:** (1) Address immediately (call COA, apologize, explain resolution), (2) Proactive quality control (weekly check-ins with families, "Is everything going well? Any concerns?"), (3) Document everything (care plans, communication, problem resolution), (4) Recover trust (offer to present to COA on "How We Ensure Quality" to demonstrate commitment), (5) Remember: Brookline families WILL complain if unhappy (affluent, educated consumers expect excellence)

**Challenge 4: "Limited parking makes caregiver visits difficult"**

**Solution:** (1) Hire caregivers comfortable with urban environment (lived/worked in cities), (2) Pay mileage/parking (don't expect caregivers to absorb costs), (3) Longer shifts (4-6 hours vs. 2-3 hours) = fewer parking challenges per shift, (4) Public transportation option (some caregivers prefer T to driving/parking), (5) Coordinate with building management (some buildings offer visitor parking passes)

**Challenge 5: "Families interview 5+ agencies—hard to differentiate"**

**Solution:** Brookline families DO research thoroughly. (1) Professional website with video testimonials (social proof), (2) COA recommendation ("The COA specifically recommended you"), (3) Jewish cultural expertise (if applicable—major differentiator), (4) Long-term care insurance billing expertise (many Brookline families have LTC insurance), (5) Demonstrate understanding of Brookline community ("We understand living in Brookline—the walkability, the community, Hebrew SeniorLife connections"), (6) Quality over volume ("We take limited clients to ensure exceptional care")
