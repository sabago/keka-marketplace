## Overview

Hospice and palliative care organizations are **valuable referral partners** for home care agencies, serving complementary roles in end-of-life and serious illness care. Massachusetts has **60+ hospice programs** providing services to 20,000+ patients annually, creating significant partnership opportunities for agencies that understand the hospice-home care relationship.

**Key distinction:**
- **Hospice provides:** Medical care (nursing, medications, medical equipment), social work, chaplaincy, end-of-life focused care
- **Home care provides:** Personal care (bathing, dressing, toileting), companionship, homemaking, overnight care

**Why hospices refer:**
- **Hospice Medicare benefit does NOT cover 24/7 personal care** (only skilled visits)
- Hospice patients need more than hospice provides: Help with ADLs, overnight supervision, caregiver respite
- **Hospices partner with home care agencies** to fill personal care gaps

---

## Why Hospice Partnerships Matter

**The hospice-home care gap:**

**What hospice DOES cover (under Medicare Hospice Benefit):**
- Nurse visits (1-3x/week typically, more if crisis)
- Hospice aide visits (2-3x/week, 1-2 hours each)
- Social worker, chaplain, volunteer visits
- All medications for hospice diagnosis
- Medical equipment (hospital bed, oxygen, wheelchair)
- 24/7 on-call nurse access

**What hospice DOES NOT cover:**
- 24/7 personal care
- Overnight caregivers
- More than 2-3 hours/day of personal care
- Homemaking, companionship beyond hospice aide visits
- **Family respite care** (family needs break, patient needs supervision)

**Where home care agencies fit:**
- Families hire home care agency to provide **additional hours** beyond hospice visits
- **Complementary care:** Hospice provides medical, agency provides personal care
- Typical scenario: Hospice aide visits 3x/week (2 hours each) + agency caregiver 30 hours/week for additional support

## Types of Hospice Referrals

### **1. Overnight Care (Highest Demand)**

**Situation:**
- Hospice patient declining (cannot be left alone, fall risk, needs repositioning overnight)
- Family exhausted (been caring 24/7 for weeks/months)
- Hospice nurse: "You need overnight help. Let me give you names of agencies we work with..."

**Home care opportunity:**
- **Overnight shift:** 8-12 hours (8pm-8am or 10pm-6am typical)
- **Duration:** 1-4 weeks (active dying phase) or longer if patient stable
- **Payment:** Private pay or long-term care insurance (NOT Medicare hospice benefit)
- **Volume:** HIGH (60-70% of hospice referrals are overnight care requests)

**Why hospices refer:**
- Overnight is biggest gap in hospice care (Medicare doesn't cover 24/7 personal care)
- Families desperately need night help (can't stay up 24/7 indefinitely)
- Hospices keep list of "agencies we trust for overnight care"

### **2. 24/7 Live-In Care**

**Situation:**
- Patient living alone (no family caregiver)
- Not safe alone even with hospice visits
- Family lives far away (California daughter managing Massachusetts parent's care)
- Hospice social worker: "Your father cannot be alone. We recommend 24/7 care."

**Home care opportunity:**
- **Live-in caregiver:** 24/7 presence (5-7 days/week)
- **Duration:** 2-8 weeks typical (end-of-life phase)
- **Payment:** Private pay (expensive, but families prioritize parent's safety)
- **Hours:** High (120-168 hours/week)

**Why hospices refer:**
- Patient alone = unsafe (hospice can't provide 24/7 presence)
- Hospice can't proceed without safety plan (live-in care solves this)

### **3. Family Respite**

**Situation:**
- Family been caregiving for months (burnout)
- Hospice social worker: "You need respite care - take a vacation, we'll arrange coverage."
- **Hospice respite benefit:** Medicare covers 5 days/year in facility OR hospices help arrange home respite

**Home care opportunity:**
- **Respite care:** 3-5 days while family away
- **Hours:** 12-24 hours/day depending on patient needs
- **Payment:** Sometimes covered by hospice respite benefit (hospice pays agency), sometimes private pay

**Why hospices refer:**
- Hospices encourage respite (prevents caregiver collapse)
- Hospices prefer home respite over facility (patient stays home)
- Agency becomes hospice's "respite care partner"

### **4. Transitional Care (Patient Not Yet on Hospice)**

**Situation:**
- Family calls hospice: "Mom is declining, we're considering hospice"
- Hospice assesses: "She's not quite ready for hospice yet (not clearly terminal within 6 months), but she needs care NOW"
- **Hospice refers to home care agency:** "Here are local agencies who can help while you decide about hospice"

**Home care opportunity:**
- Patient needs care before hospice enrollment
- Agency provides care (weeks/months)
- **Later transition to hospice:** Patient declines further, enrolls in hospice, agency continues personal care alongside hospice services

**Why hospices refer:**
- Hospices can't serve non-terminal patients (Medicare hospice eligibility requires prognosis <6 months)
- Hospices want to help families even if not hospice-appropriate yet
- Good relationships: Agency provides care pre-hospice, hospice provides medical care post-enrollment = seamless transition

## How Hospice-Agency Partnerships Work

### **Step 1: Patient Enrolled in Hospice**

- Family or physician refers patient to hospice
- Hospice nurse assesses: Medical status, prognosis, care needs, family support
- Hospice enrolls patient (Medicare Hospice Benefit or private insurance)

### **Step 2: Hospice Identifies Personal Care Gap**

Hospice team realizes:
- Hospice aide visits (6 hours/week) insufficient for patient's needs
- Patient needs overnight care (cannot be left alone)
- Family overwhelmed (need more support than hospice provides)

### **Step 3: Hospice Refers to Home Care Agency**

**Referral process:**
- **Hospice nurse or social worker:** "I'm going to give you names of home care agencies we work with..."
- Hospice provides 2-3 agency names + phone numbers
- Hospice may call agency directly: "I have a hospice patient family that needs overnight care, can I give them your contact?"

### **Step 4: Agency Coordinates with Hospice**

**Critical coordination:**
- Agency calls hospice: "We're starting care for your patient Mr. Smith, can you share care plan?"
- **Hospice provides:** Medication list, care preferences, family contact, hospice nurse contact
- **Agency provides:** Caregiver schedule, emergency contact, care notes
- **Ongoing communication:** Agency updates hospice if patient status changes

### **Step 5: Complementary Care**

**Typical weekly schedule:**
- **Monday:** Hospice nurse visit (1 hour) + agency caregiver (overnight 12 hours)
- **Tuesday:** Agency caregiver (overnight 12 hours)
- **Wednesday:** Hospice aide visit (2 hours) + agency caregiver (overnight 12 hours)
- **Thursday:** Agency caregiver (overnight 12 hours)
- **Friday:** Hospice social worker visit (1 hour) + agency caregiver (overnight 12 hours)
- **Saturday-Sunday:** Agency caregiver (overnight 24 hours)

**Hospice + agency = comprehensive care.**

## How to Build Hospice Partnerships

### **Step 1: Identify Hospice Programs in Your Service Area**

**Resources:**

**Massachusetts Hospice Organizations:**
- **Hospice & Palliative Care Federation of Massachusetts:** hospicefed.org (directory of all MA hospice programs)
- **Google Search:** "Hospice [your region]" (e.g., "Hospice Worcester," "Hospice Metrowest")
- **Medicare.gov Hospice Compare:** Compare hospice programs, find local options

**Target hospices:**
- **Regional hospices:** Hospices serving your geographic area (don't partner with Boston hospice if you serve Worcester)
- **Volume:** Larger hospices (500+ patients/year) = more referrals
- **Start with 3-5 hospices** in your service area

**Major MA Hospices:**
- Care Dimensions (largest MA hospice, serves Metro Boston/North Shore)
- HopeHealth (South Shore, MetroWest)
- Hospice of the Fisher Home (Amherst, Western MA)
- Compassionate Care Hospice (Metrowest)
- Merrimack Valley Hospice (Lowell/Lawrence area)
- Many smaller community hospices

### **Step 2: Contact Hospice Intake/Social Work**

**Outreach process:**
1. Call hospice main number
2. Ask for "intake coordinator" or "social work manager"
3. Introduce: "I'm a local home care agency owner, we'd like to be a resource for your patients and families needing additional personal care support"
4. Request meeting or phone call

**What to emphasize:**
- **Understand hospice philosophy:** "We respect hospice's role as medical provider, we complement with personal care"
- **Available for overnight care:** Most requested service
- **24/7 capability:** Weekends, holidays, emergency starts
- **Communication:** "We communicate regularly with hospice nurses"
- **Trained caregivers:** Experience with end-of-life care, comfort care, family support

### **Step 3: Demonstrate Hospice Knowledge**

**Hospices value agencies that understand:**

**A. Hospice is NOT home care:**
- Don't say: "We do the same thing as hospice"
- Do say: "We provide the personal care and companionship that complements hospice's medical care"

**B. Hospice patients are terminal:**
- Caregivers need comfort with death, dying, grief
- Training: End-of-life care, death preparation, family emotional support

**C. Hospice team is in charge:**
- Respect hospice team's medical authority
- **Agency role:** Implement hospice care plan (not create own conflicting plan)
- Communication: Report changes to hospice nurse (don't make medical decisions)

**D. Medicare Hospice Benefit limitations:**
- Understand what hospice does/doesn't cover
- Help families navigate: "Hospice covers medical care, you pay for additional personal care hours"

### **Step 4: Start Small, Build Trust**

**First hospice referral:**
- **Deliver exceptional service** (hospice's reputation on the line)
- **Communicate obsessively:** Call hospice nurse weekly with updates
- **Solve problems:** Don't bring problems to hospice without solutions
- **Be flexible:** Hospice patients decline rapidly (adjust care plans quickly)

**If you excel:**
- Hospice will refer again and again
- Hospices keep list of "trusted partners" (your goal: be on that list)

### **Step 5: Maintain Long-Term Partnership**

**Ongoing:**
- **Quarterly check-ins:** Visit hospice office, meet team, refresh business cards
- **Thank-yous:** When hospice refers, thank them (card, email, call)
- **Education:** Attend hospice's community education events (shows support)
- **Feedback:** Ask hospice "How can we serve your patients better?"

## What Hospices Look For in Home Care Partners

**1. End-of-life training:**
- Caregivers comfortable with death and dying
- Understand stages of dying (Cheyne-Stokes breathing, terminal restlessness, etc.)
- **Family support skills:** Emotional support, presence, reassurance

**2. Communication:**
- **Immediate notification:** Call hospice nurse if patient status changes
- Regular updates (hospices like to know agency caregiver is there, everything stable)
- **Collaborative:** "We're part of the hospice team" attitude

**3. Flexibility:**
- **Rapid response:** Start care within 24 hours (hospice families in crisis)
- **Adjust hours quickly:** Patient declining = increase hours, patient stabilizes = decrease
- **Weekend/holiday availability:** Death doesn't respect business hours

**4. Professionalism:**
- **Respect hospice expertise:** Don't challenge hospice medical decisions
- **Boundaries:** Agency provides personal care, hospice provides medical care (don't blur lines)
- **Documentation:** Care notes hospice can access (if needed for continuity)

**5. Reliability:**
- **Show up:** Caregiver no-shows are unacceptable (patient on hospice = critical needs)
- **Low turnover:** Continuity matters (dying patients don't want new caregivers every week)
- **Quality:** Background checks, training, supervision

## Cost and ROI

**Cost:** 100% FREE

**ROI:** Moderate to High
- **Lead quality:** Excellent (immediate need, warm referral from trusted hospice)
- **Conversion rate:** 65-80% (families desperate for help)
- **Client duration:** SHORT (2-8 weeks typical for hospice patients) BUT high hours
- **Client value:** $5k-$15k (short duration but 60-100+ hours/week = significant revenue)
- **Hours:** Very high (overnight = 60+ hours/week, 24/7 = 120-168 hours/week)
- **Payment:** Private pay or long-term care insurance (NOT Medicare hospice benefit)

**Expected referral volume:**
- 1 hospice partnership: 10-30 referrals/year
- 3 hospice partnerships: 30-90 referrals/year

**Emotional consideration:**
- Hospice work is **emotionally demanding** (caregivers see death regularly)
- **Caregiver support essential:** Debriefing, counseling, grief support for staff
- Not all agencies/caregivers suited for hospice work

## Best Practices

**1. Train caregivers on end-of-life care:**
- **Required training:** Signs of dying, comfort measures, family support, grief/loss
- **Continuing education:** Regular hospice training (many hospices offer free caregiver training)
- **Emotional support:** Debrief caregivers after patient deaths

**2. Communicate proactively with hospice:**
- **Call hospice nurse:** When you start care, when patient status changes, when caregiver has concerns
- Hospices love agencies that communicate (makes their job easier)

**3. Respect the dying process:**
- **Don't call 911:** Hospice patients have DNR orders (call hospice nurse, not 911)
- **Support family:** Dying process is emotional for families (caregiver provides presence, reassurance)
- **Allow natural death:** Hospice philosophy = comfort, not life prolongation

**4. Be available nights/weekends:**
- **Overnight care = highest demand** from hospice referrals
- If you don't offer overnight care, hospice partnerships less valuable

**5. Offer bereavement support:**
- **After patient dies:** Send card to family, offer condolences
- Families remember agencies that showed compassion
- **Future referrals:** Grieving families have other aging parents, will remember you

**6. Partner with multiple hospices:**
- Don't rely on single hospice (each hospice serves different regions)
- **3-5 hospice partnerships = stable referral flow**

**7. Understand payment sources:**
- **Private pay:** Most common (families pay out-of-pocket for extra hours)
- **Long-term care insurance:** Some policies cover hospice home care
- **NOT Medicare:** Remind families "Your additional care hours are not covered by Medicare hospice benefit"

## Common Challenges

**Challenge 1: "Families can't afford additional care hours"**
**Solution:** Hospice patients often have assets (own homes, savings). Educate families about long-term care insurance, reverse mortgages, spend-down strategies. Offer flexible arrangements (overnight only vs. 24/7). Partner with elder law attorneys for Medicaid spend-down planning.

**Challenge 2: "Caregivers uncomfortable with dying patients"**
**Solution:** Not all caregivers suited for hospice work. Recruit caregivers specifically interested in end-of-life care. Provide comprehensive training. Offer emotional support (debriefing, counseling). Don't force unwilling caregivers into hospice assignments.

**Challenge 3: "Patient died after 3 days (very short assignment)"**
**Solution:** Hospice assignments ARE short (some patients die within days). Build business model around high hours during short duration. Don't depend solely on hospice referrals (mix with long-term clients from other sources).

**Challenge 4: "Hospice nurse doesn't communicate back"**
**Solution:** Hospice nurses are overwhelmed (caseloads of 12-15 patients). Be persistent but respectful. Send updates via multiple channels (phone, text, email). Understand hospice priorities (your non-urgent questions may wait).

**Challenge 5: "Family disputes about care"**
**Solution:** End-of-life = emotional time (family conflicts common). Stay neutral: "I hear your concern, let's involve the hospice social worker." Let hospice navigate family dynamics (their expertise). Focus on patient care, not family mediation.

## Success Metrics

**Track hospice partnership performance:**
- Number of hospice partnerships (target: 3-5)
- Referrals from hospices per year (target: 30-60)
- Conversion rate (target: 70%+)
- Average hours per hospice client (target: 60-100 hours over 2-4 weeks)
- Revenue from hospice channel
- Caregiver retention (track: are hospice assignments burning out your caregivers? If yes, add support)
