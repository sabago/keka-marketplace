## Overview

Skilled nursing facilities (SNFs) are one of the **highest-volume, highest-quality referral sources** for Massachusetts home care agencies. Every year, tens of thousands of Massachusetts residents complete post-acute rehabilitation stays at nursing homes following hospitalizations for hip replacements, strokes, cardiac events, surgeries, and serious injuries. When patients are ready to discharge home, many need continued support—physical assistance, medication management, safety monitoring, exercise reinforcement—but no longer require 24/7 skilled nursing care. This creates a **natural transition point** where SNF discharge planners (social workers, case managers) refer patients to home care agencies.

**Skilled Nursing Facility (SNF) Discharge-to-Home Referral Overview:**
- **Patient population:** Post-rehab patients (hip/knee replacement, stroke, cardiac, surgery recovery, fall injuries)
- **Discharge destination:** Home with family OR independent living/assisted living
- **Home care need:** Patient ready for home but needs ongoing support (not 24/7 nursing)
- **Decision-makers:** Discharge planners (social workers), case managers, directors of social services
- **Referral mechanism:** Discharge planner contacts home care agency, family chooses from list
- **Insurance coverage:** Medicare (for skilled services), MassHealth, private pay
- **Expected volume:** 5-15 referrals/month per SNF partnership (varies by facility size, relationship depth)

**Massachusetts context:**
- **400+ nursing facilities statewide:** Mix of hospital-affiliated rehab centers, standalone SNFs, and nursing homes with rehab wings
- **Major hospital-affiliated SNFs:** Mass General Brigham Skilled Nursing Facility (Brighton), Hebrew SeniorLife centers, Spaulding Rehabilitation Hospital affiliates
- **Community SNFs:** Locally owned facilities serving specific regions (North Shore, South Shore, Western MA)
- **Massachusetts Hospital to Home Partnership Program:** $3.9M state initiative (2023-2025) to expand direct hospital-to-home discharges with HCBS support (reducing SNF utilization but increasing home care demand)
- **80% of SNF-to-home discharges occur WITHOUT home care:** Most families manage alone initially, then contact agencies weeks/months later when overwhelmed (opportunity: better discharge planner relationships capture referrals at discharge, not crisis)

**For home care agencies:**
- **High-volume source:** 5-15 referrals/month per SNF = 60-180 clients/year from one facility
- **Pre-qualified leads:** Discharge planner already assessed need, family expecting home care conversation
- **Insurance clarity:** Medicare (Part A exhausted, Part B home health possible), MassHealth, or private pay defined before discharge
- **Relationship-based:** Success requires consistent facility visits, discharge planner rapport, responsive intake
- **Competition:** Multiple home care agencies court each SNF (differentiate through responsiveness, quality)
- **Investment required:** Free to build relationships (time investment: facility visits, lunches, follow-up)

## Why SNF Partnerships Matter for Home Care Agencies

**SNF-to-home transitions represent the LARGEST single referral opportunity** for Massachusetts home care agencies. Understanding the discharge scenarios helps agencies position services appropriately.

### **Scenario 1: Post-Surgical Rehab → Home**

**Patient profile:**
- 78-year-old woman
- Total hip replacement surgery at Mass General Hospital
- 3-week rehab stay at Mass General Brigham SNF (Brighton)
- Medicare Part A SNF days exhausted (typically 20-30 days)
- **Discharge readiness:** Walking with walker, independent transfers, but needs help with dressing lower body, bathing, household tasks

**Discharge planner assessment:**
- Patient safe for home discharge (no 24/7 nursing needed)
- Family available (daughter lives nearby, works full-time)
- **Home care recommendation:** 2 hours/day, 5 days/week for 4-6 weeks

**Referral flow:**
1. **Week 2 of SNF stay:** Discharge planner meets with family, discusses discharge plan
2. **Week 3 (discharge imminent):** Discharge planner provides family with 3-5 home care agency contacts
3. **Family calls agencies:** Interviews 2-3, selects agency
4. **Discharge day:** Home care starts within 24-48 hours

**Why family needs home care:**
- Daughter works full-time (can't provide daytime care)
- Patient nervous about being alone (fall risk, new walker)
- Needs help with bathing, dressing, meal preparation during recovery

**Expected duration:** 4-12 weeks (short-term recovery), occasionally converts to long-term

### **Scenario 2: Stroke Recovery → Home**

**Patient profile:**
- 82-year-old man
- Ischemic stroke with right-side weakness
- 6-week rehab stay at Spaulding Rehabilitation Network affiliate SNF
- Significant progress: can transfer with assistance, walk short distances with walker
- **Discharge readiness:** Medically stable, but needs ADL support, safety supervision

**Discharge planner assessment:**
- Patient improving but needs ongoing exercise reinforcement, safety monitoring
- Spouse 79 years old (cannot provide all physical assistance)
- **Home care recommendation:** 4-6 hours/day, 7 days/week

**Referral flow:**
1. **Week 5 of SNF stay:** Family meeting with discharge planner, PT, OT, physician
2. **Discharge plan:** Home with home care + outpatient PT/OT continuation
3. **Discharge planner provides referrals:** 3-5 home care agencies (discharge planner: "I've worked with [Agency Name] for years, they're excellent with stroke patients")
4. **Family selects agency:** Based on discharge planner recommendation, cost, caregiver match

**Why family needs home care:**
- Physical assistance beyond spouse's capacity (transfers, bathing)
- Safety monitoring (stroke patients at fall risk)
- Exercise program reinforcement between PT/OT sessions
- Meal preparation, medication reminders

**Expected duration:** 3-6 months initially, often converts to long-term care

### **Scenario 3: Cardiac Event Recovery → Home**

**Patient profile:**
- 75-year-old woman
- Heart attack, cardiac catheterization, stent placement
- 2-week cardiac rehab stay at Hebrew SeniorLife SNF
- **Discharge readiness:** Cardiac stable, but weak, fatigued, anxious

**Discharge planner assessment:**
- Patient safe for home but needs monitoring, activity pacing, emotional support
- Lives alone (no family nearby)
- **Home care recommendation:** 2-4 hours/day initially, taper as strength improves

**Referral flow:**
1. **Week 1 of SNF stay:** Discharge planning begins (patient lives alone = home care critical)
2. **Week 2 (discharge day):** Discharge planner arranges home care start within 24 hours
3. **Home care agency:** Morning visits to assist with showering, breakfast, light household tasks, safety check

**Why patient needs home care:**
- Lives alone (no family to monitor cardiac symptoms)
- Weak and fatigued (can't manage household tasks independently)
- Anxiety about being alone after cardiac event (emotional support)
- Medication regimen complex (anticoagulants, beta blockers, ACE inhibitors—reminder support)

**Expected duration:** 4-8 weeks recovery, may continue long-term

### **Scenario 4: Fall Injury Recovery → Home**

**Patient profile:**
- 85-year-old man
- Fall at home, fractured pelvis, hospital admission
- 4-week rehab stay at community SNF (North Shore)
- **Discharge readiness:** Ambulatory with walker, pain controlled, but nervous about home safety

**Discharge planner assessment:**
- Patient improving but fall risk remains high
- Wife 83 years old (cannot physically assist if he falls)
- **Home care recommendation:** 2-3 hours twice daily (morning and evening routines)

**Referral flow:**
1. **Week 3 of SNF stay:** Discharge planning meeting (family concerned about safety)
2. **Discharge planner recommendation:** Home care agency with experience in fall prevention
3. **Family selects agency:** Interviews 2 agencies, chooses one with male caregivers available (patient preference)

**Why family needs home care:**
- Wife physically unable to assist with bathing, dressing, transfers
- Fall risk high (home care provides safety supervision)
- Confidence building (patient anxious about being home after fall)

**Expected duration:** 3-4 months initially, often extends long-term

### **Scenario 5: Waiting for Assisted Living Opening**

**Patient profile:**
- 80-year-old woman
- Pneumonia hospitalization, 3-week SNF recovery
- **Discharge plan:** Move to assisted living (family already applied)
- **Problem:** Assisted living has 2-3 month waitlist (no opening yet)

**Discharge planner assessment:**
- Patient can't return to independent living apartment alone (too frail)
- Assisted living waitlist: 8-12 weeks
- **Home care recommendation:** Bridge care until AL opening available

**Referral flow:**
1. **Week 2 of SNF stay:** Discharge planner identifies AL waitlist problem
2. **Solution:** Discharge home with intensive home care (6-8 hours/day) until AL bed opens
3. **Family agrees:** Temporary home care cheaper than extended SNF stay (private pay)

**Why family needs home care:**
- Bridge care until assisted living bed available
- Patient too frail for independent living without support
- Family can't provide 6-8 hours daily care during waitlist period

**Expected duration:** 2-3 months (until AL bed opens)

## How to Build SNF Partnerships

**SNF partnerships require consistent relationship-building** with discharge planners (social workers, case managers). Unlike hospital discharge planners who work with dozens of agencies, SNF discharge planners typically maintain relationships with 5-10 preferred home care agencies they trust.

### **Step 1: Identify Target SNFs in Your Service Area**

**Research facilities:**
- **Find SNFs near you:** MassHealth publishes nursing facility directory (mass.gov/nursing-facilities)
- **Types of SNFs to target:**
  - **Hospital-affiliated rehab centers:** Higher volume, post-acute focus (hip/knee replacements, stroke, cardiac)
  - **Community SNFs with rehab wings:** Mix of long-term residents + short-term rehab patients
  - **Standalone rehab centers:** Specialize in post-acute care (high discharge-to-home volume)

**Prioritize facilities:**
- **Geographic proximity:** Within 30 minutes of your coverage area (families prefer local agencies)
- **Discharge volume:** Larger facilities (100+ beds) = more discharges per month
- **Rehab focus:** Facilities with dedicated rehab wings = more short-term patients discharging home

**Top Massachusetts SNFs to target** (examples):
- **Mass General Brigham SNF** (Brighton): 90 beds, post-acute rehab focus
- **Hebrew SeniorLife - NewBridge on the Charles** (Dedham): Rehab and long-term care
- **Hebrew SeniorLife - Dedham Campus** (Dedham): Skilled nursing and rehab
- **Spaulding Rehabilitation Network affiliates:** Multiple locations, post-acute specialization
- **Community SNFs:** North Shore, South Shore, Worcester area (research local facilities)

**How many SNFs to target?**
- **Start:** 5-10 facilities (manageable for consistent relationship-building)
- **Goal:** 3-5 active partnerships (facilities regularly referring to you)

### **Step 2: Identify Decision-Makers (Discharge Planners)**

**Who makes referral decisions at SNFs?**
- **Discharge planners (social workers):** Primary referral gatekeepers (most common title: "Discharge Planner," "Social Services Coordinator," "Case Manager")
- **Director of Social Services:** Oversees discharge planning team, sets preferred agency list
- **Admissions Coordinator:** Sometimes involved in discharge planning (less common)

**How to find discharge planner contact info:**
1. **Call SNF main line:** "May I have the name and direct line for your discharge planner?"
2. **LinkedIn search:** "[Facility Name] discharge planner" or "[Facility Name] social worker"
3. **Visit facility:** Ask receptionist for discharge planner's business card

**What to ask for:**
- Discharge planner's full name
- Direct phone number and email
- Best time to reach them (discharge planners busy mornings, more available afternoons)

### **Step 3: Initial Outreach**

**Best approach: Phone call first, then schedule visit**

**Phone script:**
```
"Hi [Discharge Planner Name], my name is [Your Name] and I'm the [Your Title] at [Agency Name], a home care agency serving [Your Coverage Area]. We specialize in supporting patients transitioning home after rehab stays, particularly [your specialty: post-surgical, stroke recovery, cardiac, etc.]. I'd love to introduce our agency and learn about how we might support your discharge planning. Would you have 15-20 minutes for me to visit sometime in the next two weeks?"
```

**If discharge planner hesitant:**
- "I'm not looking to sell anything today—I just want to introduce myself and understand your discharge process. We're building relationships with facilities in [area] and want to be a resource for you."

**Email follow-up** (after phone call):
```
Subject: Introduction - [Agency Name] Home Care

Hi [Discharge Planner Name],

Thank you for speaking with me today. As discussed, I'd like to schedule a brief visit to introduce [Agency Name] and learn about how we can support your patients transitioning home.

About [Agency Name]:
- Serving [coverage area] since [year]
- Specializing in [post-surgical, stroke, cardiac, dementia care, etc.]
- 24/7 on-call, responsive intake (can start care within 24 hours)
- Accept Medicare, MassHealth, private pay

I'll follow up next week to schedule a visit. In the meantime, I've attached our agency brochure.

Best regards,
[Your Name]
[Phone] | [Email]
```

### **Step 4: First Facility Visit**

**When to visit:**
- **Best times:** Weekday afternoons (1-4 PM) - discharge planners less busy than mornings
- **Avoid:** Monday mornings (weekend admissions paperwork), Friday afternoons (week wrap-up)

**What to bring:**
- **Agency brochure/one-pager:** Professional, clear services overview
- **Business cards:** Bring 50-100 (leave extras for discharge planner to share with colleagues)
- **Intake packet sample:** Show how easy your intake process is (simple forms, fast turnaround)
- **Small branded item:** Pens, notepads, magnets with agency contact info (discharge planners appreciate useful items)

**Visit agenda (15-20 minutes):**

**1. Introduction (2 minutes):**
- "Thank you for meeting with me. I wanted to introduce [Agency Name] and learn about how we might support your patients discharging home."

**2. Learn about their discharge process (5 minutes):**
- "Walk me through your typical discharge planning process for patients going home."
- "What challenges do you face with home care arrangements?" (LISTEN carefully—this reveals pain points)
- "How many patients do you typically discharge home each month?"
- "What do you look for in a home care agency partner?"

**3. Position your agency as solution (5 minutes):**
- Address pain points they mentioned:
  - **If they say:** "Agencies take too long to start care" → **You say:** "We can start within 24 hours of discharge—often same-day if needed"
  - **If they say:** "Families struggle with cost" → **You say:** "We work with all insurance types and offer flexible private pay options"
  - **If they say:** "Some agencies don't communicate well" → **You say:** "We provide discharge planners with weekly updates on patient progress—you'll always know how your patients are doing"

**4. Differentiate your agency (3 minutes):**
- "What makes us different: [emphasize your strengths: local ownership, specialty expertise, bilingual caregivers, 24/7 availability, specific insurance acceptance, etc.]"

**5. Next steps (2 minutes):**
- "How would you like to proceed? May I add [Agency Name] to your preferred home care agency list?"
- "I'd like to check in monthly—may I stop by or call to see how things are going?"
- Leave materials and business cards

**After visit:**
- **Send thank-you email within 24 hours:** "Thank you for meeting with me. I'm excited to support your patients discharging home. Please feel free to call me directly anytime at [your cell phone]."

### **Step 5: Follow-Up and Relationship Maintenance**

**Immediate follow-up (within 1 week):**
- **Email discharge planner:** "Just following up after our visit last week. I wanted to confirm my contact info in case any patients are discharging home soon: [Your Name], [Cell Phone], [Email]. We can start care within 24 hours."

**Monthly check-ins (ongoing):**
- **Visit facility once per month:** "Hi [Discharge Planner], just stopping by to say hello and see if any patients might benefit from home care this week."
- **Bring coffee/treats:** Discharge planners appreciate thoughtful gestures (box of donuts, coffee tray for staff)
- **Stay visible:** Consistent presence builds trust ("I see [Your Name] here every month—I trust that agency")

**Quarterly lunch-and-learns (advanced):**
- **Offer educational session:** "We'd love to present a 30-minute lunch-and-learn on [topic: fall prevention, dementia care strategies, medication management] for your social services team"
- **Provide lunch:** Order sandwiches/salads for staff
- **Build deeper relationships:** Meet entire discharge planning team, not just one planner

**Responsive intake (critical):**
- **Answer phone immediately:** When discharge planner calls with referral, answer or return call within 30 minutes
- **Start care quickly:** Can you start care within 24 hours of discharge? This is THE most important differentiator
- **Communicate proactively:** Update discharge planner after first visit ("Just wanted to let you know we saw Mrs. Smith yesterday—she's settling in well at home")

### **Step 6: Track Referrals and Provide Feedback**

**Report back on outcomes:**
- **After first patient referral:** Call discharge planner to say thank you and provide update
  - "Hi [Discharge Planner], thank you for referring Mrs. Johnson to us. I wanted to let you know she's doing great at home—we're visiting twice daily and she's very happy with her caregiver. Thank you for trusting us with your patient."
- **Monthly:** Share outcome data (if you receive multiple referrals)
  - "We've supported 6 of your patients this quarter, and all are thriving at home. Thank you for the continued partnership."

**Address problems immediately:**
- If a patient/family is unhappy, tell discharge planner proactively and explain how you're resolving it
- Transparency builds trust ("They told me about the problem before the family called me")

## Expected Referral Volume from SNF Partnerships

**Volume depends on:**
1. **Facility size:** 50-bed SNF vs. 150-bed SNF (larger = more discharges)
2. **Rehab focus:** Rehab-focused SNFs discharge more patients home than long-term care facilities
3. **Relationship depth:** Preferred agency (top 1-3 on list) vs. one of many options
4. **Responsiveness:** Can you start care within 24 hours? (Critical differentiator)
5. **Competition:** How many other home care agencies have relationships with this SNF?

**Expected referrals per SNF partnership:**

**New partnership (months 1-6):**
- **1-3 referrals/month:** Discharge planner testing your agency with a few patients
- **Conversion rate:** 60-80% (families choose from 3-5 agency options)
- **If you deliver excellent service:** Referrals increase month-over-month

**Established partnership (months 6-12):**
- **3-8 referrals/month:** Discharge planner trusts your agency, refers more frequently
- **Conversion rate:** 70-85% (discharge planner actively recommends you: "I've worked with this agency for years—they're great")

**Preferred agency status (year 2+):**
- **8-15 referrals/month:** You're in top 1-3 agencies discharge planner recommends
- **Conversion rate:** 80-90% (strong endorsement from discharge planner)

**Typical conversion funnel:**
- 10 referrals from SNF discharge planner
- → 7 families call you for consultation (70% conversion)
- → 6 families choose your agency and start services (85% of consultations)

**Example: 100-bed community SNF with 40-bed rehab wing**
- 20-30 discharges home/month (from rehab wing)
- Your agency: 5-10 referrals/month (if preferred partner)
- Your conversion: 4-8 new clients/month from this SNF

**Revenue impact:**
- 6 new clients/month from one SNF × $4,000 average client value = $24,000/month revenue
- Over one year: 72 clients × $4,000 = $288,000 revenue from one SNF partnership

## Cost and ROI

**Investment required:**
- **Time investment:** 4-6 hours/month per SNF (visits, phone calls, follow-up)
- **Materials:** Brochures, business cards, branded items ($200-$500 annually)
- **Lunch-and-learns:** $50-$150 per session (optional)
- **TOTAL:** Minimal financial investment (primarily time and relationship-building)

**Expected ROI:**

**Year 1 (building relationships):**
- 3 SNF partnerships established
- 5 referrals/month average (across 3 SNFs)
- 60 referrals/year × 70% conversion = 42 new clients
- Revenue: $168,000 (assuming $4,000 average client value)
- **ROI:** Extremely high (minimal financial investment, time-based)

**Year 2-3 (mature partnerships):**
- 5 SNF partnerships active
- 10 referrals/month average (across 5 SNFs)
- 120 referrals/year × 75% conversion = 90 new clients
- Revenue: $360,000/year
- **ROI:** Compounding (as partnerships deepen, referrals increase)

**Break-even:**
- **One client** from an SNF partnership covers all relationship-building costs
- **2-3 referrals/month** from one SNF = extremely profitable channel

## Best Practices for SNF Partnerships

**1. Be the most responsive agency:**
- **Answer phone immediately:** Discharge planners call multiple agencies—first one to answer often gets referral
- **Start care within 24 hours:** Patients discharge home quickly (discharge planner needs agency ready NOW)
- **Provide same-day assessments:** Can you visit patient on discharge day? Huge differentiator

**2. Communicate proactively with discharge planners:**
- **Update after first visit:** "Just wanted to let you know we saw your patient yesterday—everything is going well"
- **Report concerns immediately:** If patient struggling at home, tell discharge planner right away (prevents readmissions, builds trust)
- **Monthly check-ins:** "Hi, just wanted to touch base—any patients discharging home this week?"

**3. Focus on quality, not just volume:**
- **Deliver excellent care:** Happy patients = discharge planner refers more patients
- **Address problems immediately:** If family unhappy, resolve quickly and tell discharge planner
- **Track outcomes:** Share success stories with discharge planner ("Mrs. Smith is thriving at home—thank you for the referral")

**4. Build relationships with entire social services team:**
- **Don't rely on one discharge planner:** What if that person leaves? Build relationships with 2-3 team members
- **Attend SNF events:** Some facilities host vendor appreciation events (attend to meet full team)
- **Offer educational sessions:** Lunch-and-learns on fall prevention, dementia care, medication management (positions you as expert)

**5. Understand discharge planner pressures:**
- **Regulatory compliance:** Discharge planners must offer patient CHOICE (can't force one agency)
- **Readmission penalties:** If patient readmitted to hospital within 30 days, SNF faces penalties (position your agency as preventing readmissions through quality care)
- **Family satisfaction:** Discharge planners want families happy (responsive, compassionate agencies = happy families)

**6. Differentiate from competitors:**
- **Local vs. national:** Emphasize local ownership, personalized service (vs. national chain competitors)
- **Specialty expertise:** If you specialize in post-surgical, stroke, dementia—emphasize this
- **Insurance acceptance:** "We accept all insurance types" (some agencies selective)
- **24/7 availability:** "Families can reach us anytime, day or night"

**7. Join Massachusetts Senior Care Association (MSCA):**
- **MSCA:** Trade association representing 400+ nursing facilities, ALs, CCRCs
- **Spring Conference:** Annual conference in Worcester (May) with 400+ facility administrators, discharge planners
- **Exhibit at trade show:** Meet discharge planners from across Massachusetts in one day
- **See:** [Massachusetts Senior Care Association article](/massachusetts/7-community-and-consumer-platforms/massachusetts-senior-care-association) for details

**8. Track referrals by facility:**
- **CRM/spreadsheet:** Track which SNFs refer, how many referrals/month, conversion rates
- **Identify patterns:** Which facilities refer most? Which discharge planners? (Focus efforts on high-performers)
- **Measure ROI:** Revenue from each SNF partnership (justifies continued time investment)

## Common Challenges

**Challenge 1: "Discharge planner already has preferred agencies—won't add us to list"**
**Solution:** (1) Be patient (may take 6-12 months to break in), (2) Ask: "May I check in quarterly to see if you'd be open to adding us in the future?", (3) Differentiate clearly ("We specialize in [X], which your current agencies may not offer"), (4) Focus on responsiveness ("We can start care within 24 hours—even weekends"), (5) Build relationship over time (consistent visits = eventually get a chance when existing agency disappoints)

**Challenge 2: "Families don't choose us even when we're referred"**
**Solution:** (1) Improve phone consultation (families compare agencies—how you answer phone matters), (2) Return calls faster (first agency to respond often gets client), (3) Ask discharge planner for feedback ("Have you heard why families aren't choosing us?"), (4) Offer meet-and-greet at SNF (meet family BEFORE discharge), (5) Lower price or offer flexible options (if competing on cost)

**Challenge 3: "Discharge planner refers us, but patients decline all home care"**
**Solution:** This is NORMAL. Many patients/families decline home care at discharge, then call weeks later when struggling. (1) Ask discharge planner to share your contact info even if family declines initially, (2) Follow up with family 2-4 weeks post-discharge ("Just checking in—how is Mom doing at home?"), (3) Be patient (short-term referrals often convert to long-term clients later)

**Challenge 4: "We get referrals but patients are poor fit (need 24/7 care, hospice, etc.)"**
**Solution:** (1) Clarify your services with discharge planner ("We provide non-medical home care, typically 2-12 hours/day"), (2) Ask about typical discharge scenarios (ensure alignment), (3) If you receive inappropriate referrals, explain why patient isn't a fit (educates discharge planner), (4) Refer patient to appropriate agency (hospice, skilled nursing)—discharge planner appreciates the help

**Challenge 5: "Too much competition—SNF already works with 5-10 agencies"**
**Solution:** TRUE—most SNFs work with multiple agencies. (1) Differentiate through responsiveness (be THE agency that starts care within 24 hours), (2) Specialize (post-surgical, stroke, dementia expertise), (3) Build personal relationship (discharge planner knows YOUR name, trusts YOU specifically), (4) Target underserved SNFs (smaller community facilities may have fewer agency relationships), (5) Be patient (even if you're 6th on the list initially, excellent service moves you to top 3 over time)

**Challenge 6: "Discharge planner calls us, but we can't start care fast enough"**
**Solution:** **This is the #1 reason agencies lose SNF referrals.** (1) Prioritize SNF referrals (same-day or next-day start), (2) Keep 2-3 caregivers on standby for quick-start clients, (3) Streamline intake process (can you complete intake by phone vs. requiring in-person assessment?), (4) Offer evening/weekend starts (patients discharge Fridays—can you start Saturday?), (5) If you truly can't start quickly, be honest with discharge planner and refer to another agency (builds trust for future referrals)

## Success Metrics

**Track SNF partnership performance:**
- Number of SNFs targeted (goal: 5-10)
- Facility visits per month (goal: 3-5 visits/month across all SNFs)
- Referrals received from each SNF (track monthly)
- Referral conversion rate (% of referrals that become clients) - target: 70-85%
- Revenue from SNF channel (should be 15-35% of total agency revenue for agencies prioritizing SNF partnerships)
- Preferred partner status (are you in top 3 agencies discharge planner recommends?) - track per facility
- Average time from referral to care start (goal: <24 hours)
- Patient retention (do SNF referrals convert to long-term clients?) - target: 30-50% of short-term SNF discharges extend to long-term care

**Quarterly review:**
- Which SNFs refer most frequently? (Focus continued efforts here)
- Which SNFs refer rarely/never? (Increase visit frequency or deprioritize)
- Which discharge planners are strongest advocates? (Nurture these relationships)
- Are we converting referrals at expected rate? (If not, improve intake/phone consultation)
